Keeping Your Florida Home Cool in Summer

Updated January 2026 • 7 min read

With summer temperatures regularly hitting the 90s and humidity making it feel over 100°F, keeping your Florida home cool isn't just about comfort—it's about managing significant energy costs. Here's how to stay cool efficiently.

Understanding Florida's Summer Challenge

  • Average summer highs: 90-95°F
  • Heat index: Often 100-110°F
  • Humidity: 70-90%
  • AC accounts for: 50-70% of summer electric bills
  • Average summer electric bill: $200-400+

Optimize Your Air Conditioning

Temperature Settings

  • Recommended setting: 78°F when home
  • When away: 82-85°F (don't turn off completely)
  • Each degree lower increases energy use 3-5%
  • Use programmable/smart thermostat for automatic adjustments

Fan Settings

  • Use "auto" not "on": Continuous fan reintroduces humidity
  • Ceiling fans: Allow raising thermostat 4°F without comfort loss
  • Fan direction: Counterclockwise in summer (pushes air down)

AC Maintenance

  • Change filters monthly (or per manufacturer)
  • Annual professional tune-up (spring)
  • Keep outdoor unit clear of debris
  • Clean condensate drain line (prevents clogs and water damage)
  • Ensure vents aren't blocked by furniture

AC Sizing Matters

An oversized AC cools quickly but doesn't run long enough to remove humidity. This leaves you feeling clammy even at low temperatures. Proper sizing (Manual J calculation) is essential for Florida comfort.

Block the Heat Before It Gets In

Window Treatments

  • Solar shades: Block heat while maintaining view
  • Blackout curtains: Most effective on west/south windows
  • Cellular shades: Insulating air pockets
  • Exterior shades/awnings: Block heat before it hits glass

Window Films and Upgrades

  • Low-E window film: Reduces heat gain 40-70% ($5-12/sq ft)
  • Low-E replacement windows: Long-term solution
  • Solar screens: Reduce heat gain up to 70%

Attic Improvements

Your attic can reach 150°F+ in summer:

  • Radiant barrier: Reflects heat away ($0.50-1.50/sq ft)
  • Attic insulation: R-38 minimum recommended
  • Attic ventilation: Ridge vents, soffit vents, attic fans
  • Seal air leaks: Around fixtures, ducts, and penetrations

Reduce Internal Heat Gains

Lighting

  • Switch to LED bulbs (90% less heat than incandescent)
  • Turn off lights when not in use
  • Use task lighting instead of overhead lights

Appliances

  • Run dishwasher at night
  • Use dryer in evening hours
  • Consider outdoor grilling instead of oven
  • Use microwave or Instant Pot over oven
  • Ensure refrigerator coils are clean

Electronics

  • Turn off computers and TVs when not in use
  • Unplug phone chargers and power strips
  • Consider laptop over desktop (less heat)

Landscaping for Cooling

  • Shade trees: Plant on west and south sides
  • Deciduous trees: Provide summer shade, allow winter sun
  • Shrubs: Shade AC outdoor unit (but maintain clearance)
  • Ground cover: Reduces heat reflection from bare ground

Whole-House Improvements

Light-Colored Roofing

  • Light/white roofs reflect heat
  • Can reduce attic temperature 20-40°F
  • Consider when replacing roof
  • Some HOAs have restrictions

Exterior Paint

  • Light colors on walls reduce heat absorption
  • Especially important on west and south walls

Energy-Efficient Windows

  • Look for low Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC)
  • U-factor less important in Florida than SHGC
  • Impact windows often include low-E coating

Emergency Cooling Tips

If your AC fails:

  • Close blinds and curtains immediately
  • Use box fans in windows to move air
  • Take cool showers
  • Visit air-conditioned public spaces
  • Check on elderly neighbors
  • Never leave pets or children in cars
